\section{Policy brief: Critical Thinking, Media, and Democracy in an Age
of Unchecked Technological
Development}\label{policy-brief-critical-thinking-media-and-democracy-in-an-age-of-unchecked-technological-development}

\textit{Document created 2026-04-22}

\subsection{Education against Technological
Intrusion}\label{education-against-technological-intrusion}

The ever-accelerating technological development brings convenience and
entertainment, but it also creates new avenues for exploitation and
manipulation. It may threaten democracy through the spread of unchecked,
misleading, and AI-driven information on the internet, especially on
social media. As technology is part of everyday life, it cannot simply
be rejected or ignored; it needs to be addressed through education so
that people can use it competently and understand its limitations and
impact on society.

\begin{center}
\begin{tcolorbox}[orangebox]
This brief seeks to answer the following questions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item What influences Europeans' view that \textbf{critical thinking, media, and democracy should be taught more in compulsory education} in a time of rapid technological development?
  \item What \textbf{policy recommendations can be formulated} to encourage this view?
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{center}

\subsection{The Demand for Civic
Education}\label{the-demand-for-civic-education}

\begin{flushleft}
In the CRONOS3 survey, Europeans from 11 countries were asked \textit{"Which topics would you like to see being taught more in compulsory education?"}. They could choose up to 3 answers from a list of 6 categories, one of which was \textbf{"Critical thinking, media and democracy"}. \textcolor{exactgreen}{\textbf{Across participating countries, as many as 73\% of Austrians}} and \textcolor{exactorange}{\textbf{as few as 31\% of Hungarians}} selected this option. We analyzed 21 factors related to STEM education, modern technology use, and demography to understand what drives this support.
\end{flushleft}

\subsection{Value of Normative
Orientations}\label{value-of-normative-orientations}

The most impactful factor is education:
\textbf{with every completed year the likelihood of supporting more critical thinking, media and democracy education increases by 7\%}.
A person who finishes high school is thus over 80\% more likely to see
the value of such education than a person with no formal education.
Education, which itself requires critical thinking, facilitates support
for even more training of this kind.

Next are trust in scientists and general trust in others, which increase
the likelihood of valuing this type of education by respectively
\textbf{13\% and 11\% per each step up}. Most striking, however, is the
effect of views about the changes technology will bring to society.
\textbf{Those who expect them to be negative are 67\% more likely to support an increase in critical thinking, media and democracy education}.
Given that many factors reflecting technical competence proved
non-significant, it is normative orientation --- shaped by attitude to
technology, trust, and education --- that matters most.

\begin{center}
\begin{tcolorbox}[greenbox]
Recommendation 1:

\textbf{Involve authorities from expert and scientific communities in debates on technology and its direct and indirect impact on everyday life, including both positive and negative examples (e.g. increased convenience, but also dependence).}
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{center}

\subsection{STEM Education Does Not Go Along with Critical Thinking,
Media and
Democracy}\label{stem-education-does-not-go-along-with-critical-thinking-media-and-democracy}

The most surprising results indicate that respondents who would like to
see more \textbf{"Digital tools and programming"} and
\textbf{"Maths and sciences"} in compulsory education are also
respectively \textbf{49\% and 35\% less likely} to support increased
critical thinking, media and democracy education. Respondents appear to
perceive STEM and civic-oriented education as competing paths. This may
render people pursuing more technically focused education less critical
towards technology's impact on society.

\begin{center}
\begin{tcolorbox}[orangebox]
Recommendation 2:

\textbf{Introduce in STEM education curricula more critical thinking and civic-oriented training to raise awareness of social responsibility related to technology development. Introduce technology-focused elements in civic-education curricula to increase understanding of its impacts and limitations.}
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{center}

\subsection{Engagement with Social Media
Matters}\label{engagement-with-social-media-matters}

Scrolling through social media feeds at least once a day
\textbf{decreases by 25\%} support for increased critical thinking,
media and democracy education. By contrast, reacting to social media
content (e.g.~commenting) at least once a day
\textbf{increases it by 44\%}. Engaging with and indirectly contributing
to social media raises awareness of the need for critical thinking and
civic competencies, while simple passive consumption of its content
decreases it.

\begin{center}
\begin{tcolorbox}[greenbox]
Recommendation 3:

\textbf{Access to social media should be limited and/or controlled among the youngest and relaxed over time, following training in how to safely and responsibly engage with it.}
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{center}

\subsection{The Importance of Country
Context}\label{the-importance-of-country-context}

All previous results hold regardless of country context. Nevertheless,
all countries differ significantly in their support for critical
thinking, media and democracy education compared to the reference
country --- Austria. In each country, support for this type of education
is less likely to occur.
\textbf{In Hungary the likelihood of support is 73\% lower, in Czechia 72\% lower, and in Poland and Portugal 71\% lower than in Austria.}
This indicates the importance of national context, such as the quality
and direction of debate about democracy, technology, politics, and civic
education.

\subsubsection{Analytical note}\label{analytical-note}

\begin{flushleft}
\footnotesize

We studied 21 factors of support for critical thinking, media and democracy education. Factors with no meaningful impact included: gender, age, household income, internet use frequency, posting on social media, social media attitudes, intentional digital avoidance, and ability to recognize AI-generated content.

Open data from CRONOS3 Wave 1 (\url{https://doi.org/10.21338/cronos3-w1}), ESS10 (\url{https://doi.org/10.21338/ess10e03_2}), ESS10sc (\url{https://doi.org/10.21338/ess10sce03_1}), and ESS11 (\url{https://doi.org/10.21338/ess11e03_0}) represent countries: Austria, Belgium, Czechia, Finland, France, Hungary, Iceland, Poland, Portugal, Slovenia, and the United Kingdom. A total unweighted sample of \(n = 4{,}906\) was used in the analysis.
